Can an Algorithm Predict Which Students Will Drop Out — Before It's Too Late?

I trained machine learning models on 32,000 real students from the Open University. Here is what I found — and why it matters for the future of online education.

AK
Ammara Kanwal Data Analyst · Learning Analytics Researcher · Karachi, Pakistan

One in three online students never finishes the course they enrolled in. They log in during the first week, submit an assignment or two — and then quietly disappear. No warning. No goodbye. Just an empty seat in a virtual classroom.

As a mathematics teacher and someone who has spent years thinking about how students learn, that statistic has always bothered me. Not because the number is surprising — but because so many of those dropouts could have been prevented with the right intervention at the right moment.

That question became the foundation of my machine learning project using the OULAD — the Open University Learning Analytics Dataset — one of the most comprehensive publicly available datasets on student behaviour in online education.

What is OULAD?

Released by the UK's Open University, OULAD contains anonymized records for approximately 32,000 students across multiple undergraduate modules. It captures not just final results — pass, fail, or withdrawn — but the granular digital footprint students leave behind: every click on a learning resource, every assessment submission, demographic background, and prior academic history.

What makes OULAD uniquely powerful for research is this combination of behavioural and demographic data. It lets us ask not just "who dropped out?" but "what were they doing in the weeks before they did?"

What I did

I built and compared two machine learning models — Logistic Regression and Random Forest — to predict whether a student would withdraw before completing their module. The target variable was binary: withdrawn versus not withdrawn (pass or fail both counted as "completed").

Rather than using end-of-course data, I focused on features available early in the module — the kind of signals a real institution could act on in time to make a difference. The two most predictive feature categories were:

  • VLE interactions — the total number of clicks and interactions with Virtual Learning Environment resources (lecture notes, forums, quizzes) in the early weeks of the course.
  • Assessment scores — performance on early continuous assessments (TMA scores), which proved to be among the strongest signals of eventual withdrawal.

What I found

The Random Forest model achieved up to 87% accuracy in identifying students at risk of withdrawal — significantly outperforming the Logistic Regression baseline. But the number that stayed with me was not the overall accuracy. It was what the feature importance analysis revealed:

Students who engaged less with course materials in the first two weeks were dramatically more likely to withdraw — regardless of their prior qualifications.

Low early VLE engagement was a stronger predictor of dropout than age, disability status, or even previous academic results. This challenges the common assumption that at-risk students are primarily identified by who they are — their background, their demographics. The data suggests we should be watching what they do in the first fortnight.

Why it matters

Online education has democratized access to learning in ways that were unimaginable twenty years ago. But access without completion is a hollow promise. Every student who withdraws represents not just a lost tuition fee, but a lost opportunity — for them, and for the institution that could have done more.

A model like this, embedded in a real LMS (Learning Management System), could automatically flag students showing early disengagement patterns. A tutor could receive a prompt: "Three students in your cohort have had fewer than 5 interactions this week — would you like to send them a message?" Simple. Human-led. Data-informed.

What comes next

This project is a starting point, not a conclusion. In future work I want to explore time-series modelling — treating each week as a data point rather than aggregating — and test whether intervention simulations built on this data can demonstrate real retention improvements.

My longer-term goal is to pursue a PhD in Educational Technology, specifically focused on using learning analytics to build fairer, more responsive educational systems. OULAD has given me a proof of concept. The real work is in turning that proof into practice.
